01 // PRIORITY ALGORITHM
MARCH Trauma Protocol

All combat medical care follows the MARCH algorithm. This sequence is non-negotiable — it prioritizes injuries in order of how quickly they will kill the casualty. A medic who manages a fracture before stopping arterial bleeding will lose a patient. Work the algorithm, every patient, every time.

M — MASSIVE HEMORRHAGE

Why it's first:

Uncontrolled hemorrhage is the leading cause of preventable combat death. A severed femoral artery can cause unconsciousness in under 60 seconds and death in under 3 minutes. Nothing else matters if the casualty bleeds out.

! Tourniquet application must occur within SECONDS of identifying arterial bleeding. There is no time to deliberate.

Signs of massive hemorrhage:

  • 1Pulsatile (spurting) blood — indicates arterial involvement
  • 2Large pooling blood, rapidly soaking through dressings
  • 3Traumatic amputation — treat as arterial bleed even if not actively spurting
  • 4Wound depth exposing major vessels (femoral, brachial, axillary, radial)

Treatment steps (in order):

  • 1
    Apply tourniquet — HIGH AND TIGHT

    Place 2–3 inches proximal to wound. Tighten until bleeding stops, not until it hurts. Mark time of application (TK TIME).

  • 2
    Pack wound with hemostatic gauze

    In ACM: use QuikClot or Combat Gauze. Pack tightly into the wound cavity. Apply direct pressure for 3 minutes minimum.

  • 3
    Apply pressure dressing

    Secure hemostatic packing. Do not remove once placed. Wound bridging is managed by pressure, not removal.

  • 4
    Reassess hemorrhage control

    Check for continued bleeding through dressing. If soaking through: add second layer, increase pressure, consider second TK.

  • 5
    Initiate blood transfusion if indicated

    If signs of hemorrhagic shock: establish IV access and begin transfusion. Whole blood is preferred over saline in major hemorrhage.

⚠ In ACM: tourniquets cause limb damage over time. Document time applied and communicate to higher care. Loosen only when hemorrhage is definitively controlled and patient is at a CCP or higher.

A — AIRWAY

After hemorrhage control, confirm the casualty has a patent airway. An unconscious casualty will have the tongue fall back and obstruct the pharynx. If they cannot breathe, they cannot be saved regardless of other interventions.

Signs of airway compromise:

  • 1Gurgling — fluid (blood, secretions) in the airway
  • 2Snoring / stridor — partial obstruction from tongue, swelling, or debris
  • 3Unconsciousness without other apparent cause
  • 4Paradoxical chest movement or absent respiratory effort
  • 5Facial trauma with suspected inhalation injury or swelling

Interventions — in order of invasiveness:

  • 1
    Head-tilt / chin-lift

    For unconscious casualties without suspected spinal injury. Tilts the head back to open the pharyngeal airway.

  • 2
    Jaw thrust

    Preferred if spinal injury is suspected. Displaces the mandible anteriorly without moving the cervical spine.

  • 3
    Nasopharyngeal airway (NPA)

    In ACM: insert NPA if patient is unconscious or combative and cannot maintain airway. Lubricate and insert into the right naris. Tolerated in semi-conscious patients.

  • 4
    Endotracheal intubation

    Advanced procedure. Definitive airway control. In ACM: requires advanced medic skill. Monitor for right mainstem intubation (unilateral breath sounds).

i In ACM: always position unconscious casualties in the recovery position (lateral decumbent) to prevent aspiration if intubation is not immediately available.

R — RESPIRATION

Even with a patent airway, thoracic injuries can prevent effective ventilation. Tension pneumothorax is the second most common cause of preventable combat death after hemorrhage. Identify and treat it immediately.

Tension pneumothorax signs:

  • →Absent or diminished breath sounds unilaterally
  • →Tracheal deviation (toward unaffected side — late sign)
  • →Hypoxia / cyanosis not corrected by oxygen
  • →Distended neck veins (JVD)
  • →Hemodynamic instability despite hemorrhage control
! Tension pneumothorax is a clinical diagnosis. Do NOT wait for imaging. If suspected, perform needle decompression immediately.

Treatment:

  • 1
    Seal open chest wounds (sucking chest wound)

    Apply a vented chest seal (Hyfin or Bolin) to prevent air entrainment. Vented seals allow air to escape but not enter during inspiration.

  • 2
    Needle decompression

    In ACM: 14g angiocath at 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line. Rush of air confirms tension pneumo. Leave in place; a chest tube follows when resources allow.

  • 3
    Chest tube (tube thoracostomy)

    Definitive treatment for pneumothorax/hemothorax. 5th intercostal space, anterior axillary line. Advanced procedure — PFC only.

  • 4
    Supplemental oxygen

    In ACM: maintain SpO₂ ≥ 95%. If below 90% despite airway management, suspect pulmonary contusion or significant hemothorax.

C — CIRCULATION

Having controlled hemorrhage in the M phase, the C phase focuses on restoring and maintaining perfusion. Assess for shock, establish IV/IO access, and initiate volume resuscitation using the permissive hypotension strategy.

Fluid resuscitation strategy:

✓ Target SBP: 80–90 mmHg (permissive hypotension). Higher pressures may dislodge forming clots and worsen coagulopathy.
  • 1
    Establish IV or IO access

    Two large-bore IVs (14–16g) antecubital preferred. If IV impossible: tibial IO is faster to establish than you think.

  • 2
    Fluid priority: blood first

    Whole blood > packed RBCs + FFP (1:1 ratio) > crystalloid. Saline is a distant last resort. It worsens coagulopathy and dilutes clotting factors.

  • 3
    Administer TXA early

    Tranexamic acid within 3 hours of injury. Stabilizes clot formation. Do not give TXA after 3 hours — it becomes procoagulant and worsens mortality.

H — HYPOTHERMIA & HEAD INJURY

Hypothermia — The Lethal Triad:

Trauma patients are at extreme risk for the lethal triad of death: Hypothermia + Acidosis + Coagulopathy. Each worsens the others. A cold patient cannot clot effectively; a patient who cannot clot loses blood, becomes acidotic, and dies.

⚠ Any patient who has lost significant blood is a hypothermia risk regardless of ambient temperature. Begin warming immediately after hemorrhage control.
  • 1Remove wet/blood-soaked clothing — wet clothing accelerates heat loss by 25x
  • 2Apply thermal blanket / heat-reflective wrap (hypothermia prevention blanket in ACM)
  • 3Warm IV fluids if possible — cold saline drops core temp rapidly
  • 4Protect from wind and ground contact

Head injury assessment:

Traumatic brain injury (TBI) is secondary to hemorrhage management but must be identified early. Increased intracranial pressure (ICP) can cause cerebral herniation if untreated.

i In TBI: maintain SpO₂ ≥ 95% and SBP ≥ 90 mmHg. Hypoxia and hypotension worsen TBI outcomes dramatically. Do NOT over-sedate; it masks neurological decline.

Reassessment Schedule

UNSTABLE PATIENT

Any patient in shock, with declining GCS, SpO₂ below 95%, or HR above 120. Reassess vitals every 5 minutes. Any deterioration triggers immediate re-evaluation of MARCH priorities.

STABLE PATIENT

Patient with normal vitals, controlled bleeding, patent airway, adequate perfusion. Reassess every 15 minutes. A patient can decompensate rapidly — never assume stability is permanent.

03 // HEMORRHAGE CONTROL
Blood Volume & Hemorrhagic Shock

Average adult circulating blood volume is approximately 5 liters (70 mL/kg). The body can compensate for small losses, but this compensation eventually fails — and when it does, it fails suddenly and catastrophically.

CLASS I — MILD
< 750 mL
UP TO 15% BLOOD VOLUME

Compensated. No fluid replacement required in healthy patients. Tachycardia absent or mild. Body manages through vasoconstriction and increased heart rate.

CLASS II — MODERATE
750–1500 mL
15–30% BLOOD VOLUME

Tachycardia begins. Anxiety, restlessness. BP may still be normal due to compensation. Begin crystalloid replacement. Evaluate source of hemorrhage.

CLASS III–IV — SEVERE
> 1500 mL
30%+ BLOOD VOLUME

BP falls. Confusion / altered mental status. Immediate blood transfusion. HR > 120–140. Skin pale, cool, clammy. Surgical hemorrhage control may be required.

Tourniquet Application Guide
  • 1
    Placement: HIGH AND TIGHT

    2–3 inches proximal to the wound. If in doubt, place as high on the limb as possible (high-and-tight = above all junctions). A high TK is easier to move down; a low one may not stop bleeding.

  • 2
    Tighten until bleeding stops

    Turn the windlass until active bleeding ceases. One or two clicks past pain. If bleeding continues — tighten further or apply a second TK proximal.

  • 3
    Mark the time (TK TIME)

    Write "TK" and the time on the patient's forehead or on the TK itself in permanent marker. Critical for MEDEVAC handoff.

  • 4
    Reassess every 5 minutes

    Check for breakthrough bleeding. A properly placed TK completely stops distal pulses. If you can feel a radial pulse below the TK — it is not tight enough.

! NEVER remove a tourniquet in the field. TK removal is a hospital-level decision with surgeons standing by. Converting a TK in the field causes re-hemorrhage and is fatal.
Wound Packing Technique

Junctional wounds (groin, axilla, neck) cannot be tourniqueted. Wound packing with hemostatic gauze is the primary intervention.

  • 1
    Identify the deepest point of the wound

    Do not pack superficially. The gauze must reach the bleeding source at the base of the wound.

  • 2
    Pack hemostatic gauze tightly

    Use one continuous piece, starting at the deepest point. Pack in layers, pressing firmly. No air gaps. The wound must be completely filled.

  • 3
    Apply direct pressure: 3 MINUTES minimum

    Full body weight on hands, directly on top of the wound. Three minutes. Use a timer. Releasing too early reopens the wound tract.

  • 4
    Secure with pressure dressing

    Apply an Ace wrap or pressure bandage to maintain compression. The dressing continues the job of your hands.

  • 5
    Consider junctional TK

    For groin wounds: JETT or SAM Junctional Tourniquet. For axilla/neck: wound packing is definitive.

Key Opioid Notes

Fentanyl vs Morphine: Fentanyl has a faster onset (<10s IV) and shorter duration (~16–20 min) than morphine (~21–30 min). Preferred when rapid but short analgesia is needed — e.g., prior to a painful procedure. Morphine provides longer coverage for sustained pain management.

Ketamine advantage: Unlike both opioids, ketamine does not significantly depress HR, BP, or RR at analgesic doses. Use ketamine when the patient has compromised respiratory function or is hemodynamically borderline. It is the preferred agent in hypotensive or airway-compromised casualties.

! REVERSAL: Naloxone Spray IN reverses opioid overdose (morphine & fentanyl). See Other Medications tab. Give if RR drops below 10 or patient becomes unresponsive after opioid administration.
Cardiac Arrest Drug Protocol

Shockable (VF/pVT): Defibrillate first. After 3rd shock — Amiodarone 300mg IV. If amiodarone unavailable, Lidocaine 1mg/kg IV is the alternative. Epinephrine 1mg IV every 3–5 minutes throughout.

Non-shockable (PEA/Asystole): Epinephrine 1mg IV as soon as IV/IO access is available. Repeat every 3–5 minutes. Treat reversible causes (Hs and Ts).

⚠ Amiodarone note: Can be given up to 2200mg total. Monitor for ↓BP and ↓HR as side effects — common with rapid infusion. Do not give to a patient with a pulse without confirming shockable rhythm.

Esmolol is used to rapidly reduce HR in tachyarrhythmias or post-ROSC rate control. Short acting (~5 min). Not for bradycardia or hypotension.

Adenosine terminates SVT via transient AV node blockade. Give as a rapid IV push followed by a saline flush. Duration is extremely short (<2 min). Inform the patient they will feel a brief sensation of chest pressure/doom — this is expected.

TXA — Time-Critical Window

Tranexamic acid prevents fibrinolysis (clot breakdown). It must be given within 3 hours of injury. After 3 hours, TXA becomes procoagulant and increases mortality — giving it late is actively harmful.

Dose: 1–2g IV. Onset <15 seconds. Infuse slowly — rapid push can cause hypotension. Indicated for any patient with significant hemorrhage or who is receiving blood products.

⚠ The TXA clock starts at TIME OF INJURY — not time of your arrival. Account for elapsed time before administering.
Calcium Chloride — Transfusion Protocol

Packed red blood cells and FFP are stored with citrate preservative, which chelates (binds) calcium. Giving large volumes of blood products can cause hypocalcemia, leading to hypotension and coagulopathy.

Protocol: 1g CaCl₂ IV after the first unit of blood, then 1g for every 4 subsequent units. Give slowly — rapid infusion causes bradycardia. Keep warm if possible; cold CaCl₂ causes vein irritation.

i Signs of hypocalcemia: muscle cramps, perioral tingling, carpopedal spasm, QT prolongation on ECG. Treat promptly — hypocalcemic cardiac arrest is resistant to defibrillation.

Penthrox & OTFC — Rapid Analgesia

Penthrox (methoxyflurane inhaler): Fastest acting analgesic in the kit — onset under 3 seconds. Self-administered by the patient. Limited to 8 uses per inhaler. Primarily used for short painful procedures (wound packing, splinting) or transport pain management. Does not affect hemodynamics. Not a replacement for IV analgesia in severe pain.

Fentanyl OTFC (oral transmucosal fentanyl citrate): Buccal absorption — patient rubs the lozenge against the cheek mucosa; do not chew or swallow. Onset ~2 min, peak ~10 min. Useful when IV access is not yet established. Monitor for respiratory depression. Naloxone spray is the reversal agent.

Nerve Agent Antidotes — ATNA & Midazolam

ATNA Autoinjector contains both Atropine and Pralidoxime (2-PAM). Atropine counteracts the muscarinic effects of organophosphates (SLUDGE: Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI distress, Emesis). Pralidoxime reactivates acetylcholinesterase before it "ages." Give 2–3 injections initially; titrate to drying of secretions.

Midazolam Autoinjector is given concurrently to terminate nerve-agent-induced seizures. Benzodiazepines are first-line for organophosphate seizures. Monitor airway after injection — midazolam causes respiratory depression and hypotension. Position in recovery position if unmonitored.

! Nerve agent treatment sequence: ATNA first (2–3 injections) → Midazolam if seizures present → Reassess secretions → Additional ATNA if needed. Do not wait for a full symptom picture — treat early.
Naloxone — Opioid Reversal

Naloxone (Narcan) is a competitive opioid antagonist. It rapidly reverses respiratory depression, sedation, and analgesia from morphine, fentanyl, and all other opioids. Duration is ~6 minutes — shorter than most opioids.

⚠ Renarcotization risk: Naloxone wears off before the opioid does. A patient who wakes up may re-sedate 6–10 minutes later. Repeat dosing may be required. Monitor continuously after administration.

Administer if: RR < 10, patient unresponsive after opioid administration, SpO₂ declining without airway cause. Pinpoint pupils + respiratory depression + opioid history = classic triad.

Ammonia Inhalant — Patient Arousal

Ammonia inhalants (smelling salts) cause a reflexive withdrawal response that briefly increases sympathetic tone — HR and arousal increase transiently. Used to rouse a briefly unconscious or stunned patient.

i Ammonia inhalants do not treat the underlying cause of unconsciousness. If a patient does not rouse, or rouses only briefly before becoming unresponsive again, a serious cause (TBI, hemorrhagic shock, airway) must be urgently identified.

Do not use on patients with facial trauma, suspected inhalation injury, or known airway compromise. The involuntary inhalation can worsen airway swelling.

Cardiac Arrest Algorithm (ACM Protocol)
  • 1
    Confirm cardiac arrest

    No carotid pulse for 10 seconds. Unresponsive, apneic. Call it.

  • 2
    Begin high-quality CPR

    100–120 compressions/min. Depth: 5–6 cm. Full chest recoil. Minimize interruptions. 30:2 ratio (unventilated) or continuous with airway secured.

  • 3
    Secure the airway

    BVM ventilation with high-flow O₂ during CPR. Intubate when able without stopping compressions.

  • 4
    Epinephrine 1 mg IV/IO q3–5 min

    Give after second cycle of CPR. Repeat every 3–5 minutes throughout resuscitation.

  • 5
    Identify reversible causes (Hs and Ts)

    Simultaneously diagnose and treat while CPR continues. In trauma: rule out tension pneumo and massive hemorrhage first.

  • 6
    Reassess rhythm every 2 minutes

    Minimize pause to < 10 seconds. Shockable (VF/pVT): defibrillate 200J biphasic, resume CPR immediately. Non-shockable (PEA/asystole): continue CPR, treat reversible causes.

  • 7
    Monitor EtCO₂ for ROSC

    EtCO₂ < 10 during CPR = poor prognosis. Sudden rise to > 40 = ROSC. Check pulse immediately.

  • 8
    Post-ROSC care

    SpO₂ 94–98%, SBP ≥ 90, identify and treat cause. Start targeted temperature management if unconscious.

⚠ Traumatic cardiac arrest rule: In penetrating trauma, resuscitative thoracotomy (open cardiac massage) is indicated within 15 minutes of witnessed arrest. In blunt trauma, survival from arrest is extremely low. Consider cessation of efforts after 10 minutes of CPR with no reversible cause found and no ROSC.

CASEVAC vs. MEDEVAC

CASEVAC

Non-medical vehicle. Any available asset — truck, helicopter, APC. No medical personnel or equipment onboard. Used when MEDEVAC is unavailable or situation demands immediate extraction over medical care.

MEDEVAC

Dedicated medical evacuation. Protected under Geneva Convention — marked with red cross. Medical personnel and equipment onboard. MEDEVAC aircraft cannot engage the enemy without losing protected status.

Patient Handoff (MIST Report)

When transferring care to MEDEVAC crew or higher echelon:

  • M
    Mechanism of injury

    GSW, blast, blunt trauma, fall. Direction, distance, caliber if known.

  • I
    Injuries sustained

    List all known injuries from head to toe. Note suspected injuries not yet confirmed.

  • S
    Signs and vital signs

    Current HR, BP, SpO₂, GCS, RR, temperature. Trend — improving or deteriorating?

  • T
    Treatment administered

    Everything given: tourniquets (TIME), medications (dose, route, time), IV access, procedures. TK time is mandatory.

Every medical decision must answer these three questions — in order, every time:

  • 1
    What is killing this patient right now?

    Is it hemorrhage? Airway obstruction? Tension pneumo? Cardiac arrest? Identify the most immediate threat to life before doing anything else.

  • 2
    What can I fix immediately?

    What do you have? What skills do you have? What interventions are within your capability at this location and with these resources? Do those things first.

  • 3
    Is the patient improving or deteriorating?

    After every intervention, reassess. If deteriorating despite treatment — you missed something. Go back to the beginning of MARCH.

⚠ A medic who doesn't reassess is not treating a patient — they're treating a snapshot. Patients change. Stay vigilant.
08 // REFERENCE
Quick Reference Guides
Prolonged Field Care (PFC) Protocol ▶

PFC is initiated when evacuation is delayed beyond 60 minutes. Continuous monitoring and aggressive supportive care prevent preventable death in the field.

PFC Goals:

  • →Definitively control hemorrhage (all wounds packed, TKs assessed)
  • →Maintain patent, protected airway — intubate or NPA if required
  • →Prevent hemorrhagic shock — SBP 80–90 mmHg
  • →Aggressive pain management — ketamine preferred
  • →Antibiotic prophylaxis — prevent wound infection
  • →Hypothermia prevention — warm, dry, shielded from elements
  • →Maintain urine output ≥ 0.5 mL/kg/hr (monitor if Foley placed)

Monitoring Schedule:

PATIENT STATUSREASSESS INTERVAL
UnstableEvery 5 minutes
ImprovingEvery 10 minutes
StableEvery 15 minutes

Documentation during PFC: Every vital sign, every medication, every procedure, every time. This follows the patient to higher care and informs decisions at the next echelon. Without documentation, you are starting from zero.

Intraosseous (IO) Access ▶

Intraosseous access provides a non-collapsible vascular route in cardiac arrest or severe shock where peripheral IVs have failed. IO delivers drugs and fluids as fast as IV.

Common IO sites:

  • 1
    Proximal tibia (preferred)

    2 cm below and medial to the tibial tuberosity. Flat medial surface. Largest marrow space. Fastest flow rates.

  • 2
    Proximal humerus

    Greater tubercle. Higher flow rates than tibia. Preferred for large volume resuscitation. Requires arm adduction.

  • 3
    Sternal (FAST1)

    Manubrium. Only in adults. Largest marrow space but requires dedicated sternal device.

IO procedure (EZ-IO drill):

  • 1Identify landmark and prepare site (betadine/chlorhexidine)
  • 2Select needle size: 15 mm (standard adult), 25 mm (obese)
  • 3Stabilize limb. Insert at 90° to bone with moderate pressure
  • 4Drill until decreased resistance ("give") felt
  • 5Confirm: flush 10 mL NS, no swelling, low resistance
  • 6Infuse lidocaine 40 mg IO before drug/fluid administration (for conscious patient)
  • 7Use pressure bag ≥ 300 mmHg for fluid boluses — passive drip is too slow
EtCO₂ Monitoring & Interpretation ▶

End-tidal CO₂ (capnography) provides continuous, real-time feedback on ventilation AND perfusion. It is the most sensitive indicator of ROSC during CPR and the best tool to confirm correct intubation tube placement.

EtCO₂ VALUEINTERPRETATIONACTION
35–45 mmHgNormal ventilation and perfusionContinue current management
< 35 mmHgHyperventilation or reduced perfusionReduce RR, reassess perfusion
> 45 mmHgHypoventilation / CO₂ retentionIncrease ventilatory rate/volume
< 20 mmHgVery poor perfusion / CPR in progressImprove CPR quality, treat reversible causes
10–20 mmHgCardiac arrest range during CPRContinue CPR, epinephrine

Waveform interpretation:

A normal capnography waveform has four distinct phases. Loss of the characteristic shape indicates a problem:

  • →Absent waveform: tube in esophagus, disconnection, no cardiac output
  • →Shark-fin shape: bronchospasm or COPD — expiration is prolonged
  • →Declining baseline: CO₂ absorber exhausted or calibration error
  • →Oscillating baseline: cardiac oscillations — common in brady patients
✓ Intubation confirmation: consistent EtCO₂ waveform through 6 breaths confirms tracheal placement. EtCO₂ is more reliable than watching chest rise or listening to breath sounds.
Mass Casualty Triage (START / SALT) ▶

When casualties outnumber medics, triage determines the order of treatment. The goal is the greatest good for the greatest number. Individual patient care gives way to population-level care.

START Triage (30-second assessment):

  • 1
    Walk? → GREEN (Minor)

    If ambulatory, tag green. Send them to collect point. They wait.

  • 2
    Breathing?

    If not breathing: open airway. Still not breathing → BLACK (Expectant). Breathing after maneuver → RED.

  • 3
    Resp Rate > 30 or < 10?

    Yes → RED. Rate outside range is a critical sign.

  • 4
    Radial pulse absent or cap refill > 2 sec?

    Yes → RED (control hemorrhage, move on).

  • 5
    Cannot follow simple commands?

    Yes → RED. No → YELLOW (Delayed).

Triage Tags:

RED Immediate. Life-threatening, survivable with immediate care. Treat first.
YELLOW Delayed. Serious but can wait. Will not immediately die without treatment.
GREEN Minor. Walking wounded. Self-care or buddy care. Last priority.
BLACK Expectant. Non-survivable injuries OR no resources to save. Comfort care only.
! The hardest skill in MASCAL is tagging someone BLACK. An expectant patient with scarce resources will cost lives saved. This is doctrine — not abandonment.

IV Access & Fluid Resuscitation

Establishing vascular access is a fundamental skill. In a patient who is bleeding out, every second without IV access is a second without volume replacement. Know the sites, know the technique, know when to escalate to IO.

Peripheral IV Access — Site Selection
SITEGAUGEFLOW RATENOTES

Forearm16–18gGoodBackup if AC unavailable. Cephalic and basilic veins.

IV Catheter Size & Flow

Flow rate through a catheter is governed by Poiseuille's Law — flow increases with the 4th power of the radius. A 14g catheter flows dramatically faster than an 18g.

✓ In trauma: always use the largest bore catheter you can place. Upsizing from 18g to 14g gives you 5x the flow rate. In a hemorrhage patient this is the difference between keeping up with losses and falling behind.
Blood Product Administration

In ACM, whole blood and blood products are the gold standard for hemorrhagic shock resuscitation. Saline and crystalloids dilute clotting factors and worsen the lethal triad.

  • 1
    Whole Blood (WB) — First choice

    Contains RBCs, plasma, and platelets. Best 1:1:1 ratio by default. Replaces what the patient is losing. Not always available — know your kit inventory.

  • 2
    Packed Red Blood Cells (pRBC) + Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP)

    Give in a 1:1 ratio. RBCs carry oxygen; FFP replaces clotting factors. Without FFP, transfusing RBCs alone dilutes clotting factors further.

  • 3
    Normal Saline / Hartmann's — Last resort

    Appropriate for Class I–II hemorrhage as a bridge. In Class III–IV, crystalloids worsen coagulopathy, dilute clotting factors, and cause hyperchloremic acidosis. Use minimum volume necessary to maintain SBP 80–90.

i Calcium Chloride protocol: Give 1g IV after the first unit of blood, then 1g per every 4 units thereafter. Citrate in stored blood chelates calcium — hypocalcemia causes cardiac dysfunction and worsens coagulopathy.
Fluid Rates & Permissive Hypotension

Traditional "flood the patient" resuscitation is harmful in uncontrolled hemorrhage. Modern damage control resuscitation targets permissive hypotension — keeping the patient just perfusing until hemorrhage is surgically controlled.

PATIENT CONTEXTTARGET SBPRATIONALE
Hemorrhagic shock, uncontrolled bleeding80–90 mmHgHigher pressure blows out forming clots
Traumatic brain injury (TBI)≥ 90 mmHgBrain requires higher perfusion pressure
Post-hemorrhage control / stable≥ 90 mmHgNormal resuscitation target once bleeding stopped
Penetrating cardiac injury60–70 mmHgLowest survivable — keep perfusing vital organs only
! Never use permissive hypotension in TBI patients. They require higher cerebral perfusion pressure. If a patient has both TBI and hemorrhage — target ≥ 90 SBP and control hemorrhage faster.
10 // AIRWAY PROCEDURES
Advanced Airway Management

A definitive airway is a cuffed tube in the trachea. Everything else is temporary. Know the hierarchy of airway interventions — when to escalate, and when a failed airway means you go surgical.

Nasopharyngeal Airway (NPA)

BASIC AIRWAY — SEMI-CONSCIOUS PATIENTS

  • 1Size: measure from tip of nose to earlobe (~6–8 cm in adults)
  • 2Lubricate with water-soluble gel before insertion
  • 3Insert into the right naris (left if right is obstructed), bevel toward the nasal septum
  • 4Advance gently with a slight rotating motion — never force
  • 5Correct depth: patient tolerates without gagging, air moves freely
  • 6Flange must rest against the nostril — prevents aspiration into airway
⚠ Contraindicated in suspected basilar skull fracture (periorbital bruising, Battle's sign, clear CSF from nose/ears). Risk of intracranial placement.
Endotracheal Intubation (ETI)

DEFINITIVE AIRWAY — UNCONSCIOUS / RSI

  • 1
    Pre-oxygenate

    3–5 minutes high-flow O₂ via BVM or NRB mask. Buys time during the procedure.

  • 2
    RSI medications (if conscious)

    Ketamine 1.5 mg/kg IV (induction) + Succinylcholine 1.5 mg/kg IV (paralytic). Paralytic onset 45–60 sec.

  • 3
    Laryngoscopy

    Left hand, direct laryngoscopy. Blade in vallecula (Mac) or behind epiglottis (Miller). Lift — don't lever.

  • 4
    Pass tube

    7.5–8.0 mm ID for adult male, 7.0–7.5 mm for female. Watch tube pass cords. Advance to 21–23 cm at teeth.

  • 5
    Inflate cuff

    5–10 mL air. Cuff pressure 20–30 cmH₂O if manometer available.

  • 6
    Confirm placement

    EtCO₂ waveform (gold standard). Bilateral breath sounds. Chest rise. No gastric sounds over epigastrium. Secure tube.

Surgical Cricothyrotomy

CANNOT INTUBATE / CANNOT OXYGENATE

The last resort in a "can't intubate, can't oxygenate" (CICO) scenario. Every second of hypoxia is brain damage. If you've failed two intubation attempts and a supraglottic airway — cut.

  • 1Identify the cricothyroid membrane — midline, between thyroid and cricoid cartilage
  • 2Stabilize the larynx with non-dominant hand. Laryngeal handshake: thumb and middle finger on thyroid, index on CTM.
  • 3Horizontal stab incision through skin and CTM (1–1.5 cm). A vertical skin incision first gives better exposure.
  • 4Hook tracheal hook caudally to retract the inferior tracheal wall and prevent loss of hole
  • 5Insert 6.0 mm cuffed tube, inflate cuff, confirm with EtCO₂
  • 6Secure tube. Revert to formal tracheostomy within 72 hours.
! In ACM: surgical cric is an advanced procedure requiring high skill level. Do not hesitate — hypoxia kills faster than a botched cric. A misplaced tube is recoverable. A dead patient is not.
BVM Ventilation Technique

Bag-valve-mask ventilation is the bridge between no airway and a definitive airway. Done poorly, it causes gastric inflation, aspiration, and worsens outcomes.

  • 1
    Two-provider technique is always preferred

    One provider creates a seal (two-hand EC-grip), one squeezes the bag. One-provider BVM produces a poor seal and inadequate volumes.

  • 2
    Tidal volume: 500–600 mL (6–7 mL/kg)

    Visible chest rise only. Over-ventilation causes gastric inflation and barotrauma. Squeeze the bag gently over 1 second — do not slam it.

  • 3
    Rate: 10 breaths/min (1 breath every 6 sec)

    Hyperventilation causes hypocapnia, cerebral vasoconstriction, and worsens TBI. In cardiac arrest: do not over-ventilate during CPR.

  • 4
    OPA + NPA for unconscious patients

    Insert an oropharyngeal airway (OPA) to lift the tongue and maximize airway patency. Size: corner of mouth to earlobe.

Failed Airway Algorithm

A structured decision tree prevents panic-driven escalation errors when airway attempts fail.

  • 1
    Attempt 1: Direct laryngoscopy

    Optimize: position (ear-to-sternal notch), BURP maneuver (Backward, Upward, Rightward Pressure on thyroid), suction clear. Max 30 sec.

  • 2
    Failed attempt 1 → Call for help + Maintain O₂

    BVM ventilation between attempts. Do not rush attempt 2 with a desaturating patient — stabilize SpO₂ first.

  • 3
    Attempt 2: Change something

    Different blade, different size tube, video laryngoscopy if available, bougie-assisted intubation, different operator.

  • 4
    Failed attempt 2 → Supraglottic Airway (SGA)

    i-gel or LMA. Blindly inserted, does not require visualization. Buys time while preparing for cric.

  • 5
    Cannot ventilate with SGA → Surgical Cric

    CICO declared. No further attempts at intubation. Cricothyrotomy now.

! Two failed intubation attempts is the limit in trauma. Beyond two attempts, airway edema and trauma from the scope make subsequent attempts less likely to succeed, not more.

Wound Management & Infection Control

Combat wounds are universally contaminated. Dirt, clothing fragments, bone, and environmental debris are driven into tissue by ballistic and blast injuries. Infection is a leading cause of late-stage combat death and limb loss — prevention starts at point of injury.

Wound Classification

GSW — High velocityRifle, carbine (>600 m/s)High — cavitation damagePack, dress, urgent evac, antibiotics

Wound Irrigation

Irrigation is the single most effective measure against wound infection. Mechanical removal of debris and bacteria outperforms any antibiotic when applied properly.

  • 1
    Volume: minimum 1 liter per wound zone

    High-energy wounds require 3–5 liters. Normal saline is preferred. Potable water is acceptable in austere settings — sterile solution is better but delayed irrigation is worse.

  • 2
    Pressure: 5–8 psi (pulsatile lavage)

    Use a 35 mL syringe with an 18g angiocath or a pressure irrigation device. Low-pressure pouring is inadequate — pulsatile flow dislodges biofilm and debris.

  • 3
    Remove visible debris

    Forceps or gloved finger — remove fragments, clothing, dirt visible in the wound. Do not probe blindly for bullets — tracking injury is a surgical job.

  • 4
    Do not close contaminated wounds

    Combat wounds should NOT be primarily closed in the field. Leave open, dress loosely. Premature closure traps contamination and creates anaerobic conditions ideal for gas gangrene.

Antibiotic Protocol (Ertapenem)

Ertapenem is a broad-spectrum carbapenem antibiotic covering gram-positive, gram-negative, and anaerobic organisms — the profile that matters in combat wounds.

PARAMETERVALUE
Dose1g IV or IM
FrequencyOnce daily
Route (IV)Infuse over 30 minutes
Route (IM)Mix with 3.2 mL 1% lidocaine — reduces injection pain
IndicationAny contaminated wound, blast/fragmentation injury, open fracture
Evacuation triggerWound infection requires evacuation to surgical care
⚠ Ertapenem does NOT cover Pseudomonas or MRSA. If wound infection is progressing despite ertapenem — escalate to higher medical care immediately. Field antibiotics are prophylaxis, not treatment for established infection.

Timing matters: Give antibiotics within 3 hours of injury for maximum prophylactic effect. The "golden window" for preventing wound infection mirrors the TXA window. Both should be given together in major trauma.

Splinting & Fracture Management

Fractures in the field are managed conservatively — immobilize, align, and evacuate. Attempting reduction of complex fractures without surgical backup risks vascular injury and compartment syndrome.

  • 1
    Assess neurovascular status BEFORE and AFTER splinting

    Check distal pulse, capillary refill, sensation, and motor function. Document findings. Loss of distal pulse after splinting = vascular emergency.

  • 2
    Traction splint for femur fractures

    Femur fractures can sequester 1–2 liters of blood into the thigh. A traction splint (Sager or Hare) reduces the fracture space and controls internal hemorrhage. Apply traction until pain reduces ~50%.

  • 3
    Splint in position of function

    Wrist: slight extension. Ankle: 90° neutral. Knee: slight flexion. Immobilize the joint above and below the fracture.

  • 4
    Open fractures: do NOT reduce

    Cover bone with a moist, sterile dressing. Do not push bone back in — this introduces contamination deeper. Irrigate the wound around the fracture. IV antibiotics mandatory.

  • 5
    Compartment syndrome signs

    The 6 Ps: Pain out of proportion, Pressure (tense compartment), Paresthesia, Paralysis, Pallor, Pulselessness (late). If suspected — remove all circumferential dressings/splints. This is a surgical emergency.

Burns and environmental injuries are secondary to hemorrhage and airway management in the initial primary survey — but they become primary causes of morbidity and mortality in prolonged field care. Manage them properly from the start.

Rule of Nines — BSA Estimation

The Rule of Nines estimates total body surface area (TBSA) burned. This drives fluid resuscitation calculation (Parkland formula).

BODY REGION% TBSA (ADULT)

⚠ Burns > 20% TBSA require IV fluid resuscitation. Burns > 40% TBSA are potentially fatal and require immediate evacuation to a burn center.
Burn Wound Management
  • 1
    Stop the burning process

    Remove clothing and jewellery from the burn area. Irrigate with cool (NOT cold or ice) water for 20 minutes. Cold causes vasoconstriction and worsens depth. Hypothermia is a real risk — cool only the burn, not the patient.

  • 2
    Assess airway urgently in facial/inhalation burns

    Signs of inhalation injury: singed nasal hairs, carbonaceous sputum, hoarse voice, stridor, facial burns. Airway edema develops over hours — intubate early before the window closes. A swollen airway cannot be intubated.

  • 3
    Cover burns with appropriate dressings

    Non-adherent dressings (Mepitel, cling film as a field expedient). Do NOT use dry gauze — it sticks and causes secondary damage on removal. Do NOT burst blisters — they are a sterile biological dressing.

  • 4
    Analgesia — burns are extraordinarily painful

    Ketamine IV/IM for dressing changes and procedures. Morphine or fentanyl for sustained pain. Paracetamol as adjunct. Circumferential burns need escharotomy — this is a surgical procedure.

13 // FIELD PROCEDURES
Critical Field Procedures

These procedures are potentially lifesaving and within the scope of a trained corpsman. They require practice, correct identification of anatomy, and confidence under pressure. Hesitation costs lives.

Needle Thoracostomy (Needle Decompression)

TENSION PNEUMOTHORAX — IMMEDIATELY LIFE-THREATENING

Indications:

  • →Absent unilateral breath sounds + penetrating chest trauma
  • →Respiratory distress + hypotension not responding to fluids
  • →Tracheal deviation (late sign)
  • →JVD + hypotension + absent breath sounds (Beck's triad of pneumo)
  • →Cardiac arrest in trauma with suspected tension pneumo — decompress BEFORE resuming CPR

Procedure:

  • 114g angiocath, 8 cm length minimum (standard 3.25 cm needles fail in 50%+ of patients)
  • 2Site: 2nd ICS, midclavicular line. Advance over the superior border of the rib (neurovascular bundle runs inferior)
  • 3Insert perpendicular to skin until a rush of air is heard/felt. Advance catheter, remove needle.
  • 4Leave catheter in place — do not cap. Monitor for re-tension.
  • 5Alternate site: 4th–5th ICS, anterior axillary line — higher success rate in muscular/obese patients
✓ Success: rush of air, immediate improvement in BP and SpO₂, trachea re-centres. Reassess every 5 minutes — the catheter can kink or clot. If patient deteriorates again, repeat or proceed to finger thoracostomy.
Chest Seal Application (Open Pneumothorax)

SUCKING CHEST WOUND — TREAT WITHIN SECONDS

An open chest wound larger than 2/3 the diameter of the trachea will preferentially draw air through the wound rather than the trachea on each breath. The lung collapses. Seal immediately.

  • 1
    Wipe the skin dry

    Vented chest seals (Hyfin, Bolin) require a dry surface for adhesion. Blood and sweat will cause seal failure. Wipe aggressively.

  • 2
    Apply seal centered over wound

    Do not pack the wound before applying the seal — leave the tract open for the seal's flutter valve to function.

  • 3
    Vented seals only — in ACM and in doctrine

    Vented (flutter valve) seals allow air to escape during exhalation but prevent air entry during inspiration. Non-vented (occlusive) seals can convert an open pneumo to a tension pneumo — more dangerous than the original wound.

  • 4
    Apply seal to BOTH wounds

    High-velocity rounds almost always have both an entry and an exit wound. Both must be sealed. Sealing only one creates a tension pneumo from the other.

  • 5
    Monitor and burp if tension develops

    If the patient deteriorates after sealing — lift one corner of the seal to allow trapped air to escape (burp the seal). If tension persists — needle decompression.

Pericardiocentesis (Cardiac Tamponade)

ADVANCED PROCEDURE — PFC / SURGICAL LEVEL

Cardiac tamponade occurs when blood accumulates in the pericardial sac, compressing the heart. Even small volumes (150–200 mL) can be fatal. Diagnosed clinically by Beck's Triad.

⚠ Beck's Triad: (1) Hypotension + (2) Jugular Venous Distension (JVD) + (3) Muffled/distant heart sounds. In trauma with penetrating chest injury, assume tamponade until proven otherwise.
  • 118g spinal needle, 10 cm, attached to a 60 mL syringe
  • 2Site: subxiphoid approach — 45° angle toward the left shoulder, just below and left of the xiphoid
  • 3Advance slowly, aspirating continuously. ECG monitoring if available — ST changes indicate cardiac contact
  • 4Withdraw non-clotting blood (pericardial blood defibrinated — does not clot). Even 20–30 mL removal dramatically improves BP
  • 5Leave catheter in situ for repeat aspiration. Definitive treatment is surgical pericardial window.
Urinary Catheterisation (PFC)

PROLONGED FIELD CARE — MONITORING RENAL PERFUSION

In PFC beyond 2–3 hours, urinary output becomes a critical monitoring parameter. Urine output is a direct proxy for renal perfusion, which reflects overall circulatory adequacy.

Target urine output: 0.5 mL/kg/hr (35 mL/hr for a 70 kg patient). Output below this threshold indicates inadequate fluid resuscitation or ongoing hemorrhage.

  • 1
    Contraindications first

    Suspected urethral injury: blood at the meatus, perineal hematoma, high-riding prostate, pelvic fracture. Do NOT catheterise — suprapubic route or urology consult.

  • 2Strict aseptic technique. Foley catheter 14–16 Fr. Lubricate generously.
  • 3Advance until urine returns. Inflate balloon only after urine flows — premature inflation in the urethra causes severe trauma.
  • 4Record every void — time, volume, colour. Dark amber/brown = haematuria or dehydration. No urine = failed placement or renal failure.
